American Dream

With a deeper understanding of both characters' psyches, how are they used to highlight certain themes or ideas? In Breaking Bad and Death of a Salesman, two themes that they both display effectively through their protagonists are the American Dream and toxic masculinity.

First, let's begin with the American Dream, the ideal that every person has an equal opportunity to succeed. The American Dream means different things to different people, but generally, it represents success and happiness, a path that leads to prosperity and fulfillment. Both Breaking Bad and Death of a Salesman illustrate the consequences of pursuing the American Dream, using Walter White and Willy Loman's obsession with success to show its darker side.

Walter White's American Dream

The American Dream's idea of success and social validation is a driving force in Walter White's life. Walter White's concept of the American Dream revolves around self-made wealth, respect, and power. He doesn't just want to make money for his family; he wants to be recognized and remembered. This ambition starts out as a quest for financial security but gradually evolves into an intense hunger for power and dominance. In his attempt to secure the American Dream, Walter goes from a meek, downtrodden teacher to a feared drug kingpin. This pursuit of success at all costs reflects the corruption of the American Dream. Walter's relentless drive for power and status shows how ambition, when untethered, can transform into greed and entitlement.

Willy Loman's American Dream

Willy Loman's American Dream is more about reputation and social standing than financial gain alone. He believes that being "well-liked" and admired is key to success, a belief that has shaped his entire life. Willy's version of the American Dream is grounded in the idea that charm and popularity are as essential to success as hard work. Throughout the play, Willy repeatedly tells his sons that if they are "well-liked," they will be successful. This belief blinds him to the fact that he's losing his job and has little actual financial security. Willy clings to the idea that superficial qualities like charisma are enough to achieve success, and he overlooks his sons' actual needs and talents.

Both Walter and Willy demonstrate how the American Dream can become toxic when it transforms from a dream of opportunity to an obsessive desire for status. This obsession causes both characters to prioritize their egos and ambitions over the well-being of their families. Walter's and Willy's devotion to the American Dream ultimately harms their relationships and erodes their sense of self. By pursuing an idealized vision of success, they lose touch with the original, more innocent goals they had for themselves and their families.

Toxic Masculinity

Both characters are also affected by another destructive force: toxic masculinity. Toxic masculinity involves adhering to traditional male stereotypes like toughness, emotional restraint, and a need for dominance. This concept can distort a person's sense of identity, forcing them to suppress vulnerabilities in favor of aggressive or controlling behaviors.

Walter White's Toxic Masculinity

Walter White's descent into Heisenberg is largely fueled by toxic masculinity. Feeling inadequate as a teacher and emasculated by his financial dependence, Walter compensates by asserting dominance in the drug world. When he first enters the meth business, he claims it's to provide for his family, but over time, it becomes clear he's driven by ego. The success of Heisenberg gives him a newfound sense of power and control, something he lacked in his life as a teacher. His transformation embodies toxic masculinity, as he sacrifices moral integrity and justifies increasingly violent acts to maintain his status.

Willy Loman's Toxic Masculinity

Willy Loman's toxic masculinity manifests in his stubborn pride and refusal to accept help. He constantly upholds his image as a proud, independent man, despite being unable to fulfill his financial obligations. He often dismisses his own insecurities by belittling others and refuses help even when he desperately needs it. Willy's fragile ego and need for control push him to make reckless decisions that further damage his relationship with his family.

The toxic masculinity in both characters ultimately contributes to their downfall. Walter's identity as Heisenberg drives him deeper into crime, alienating him from his family and turning him into someone they can't recognize. Willy's pride and need for validation lead him into a tragic spiral of denial and self-destruction. Both Breaking Bad and Death of a Salesman use toxic masculinity to illustrate the dangers of societal expectations and the pressures of conforming to rigid gender roles.

Conclusion

In Breaking Bad and Death of a Salesman, we witness two men whose lives are destroyed by a pursuit of ideals that society has conditioned them to desire. Walter White's transformation into Heisenberg and Willy Loman's tragic spiral are cautionary tales of how the American Dream, combined with toxic masculinity, can warp a person's sense of self and lead to their downfall.

These stories serve as reflections on the dangers of unchecked ambition and societal pressure. Ultimately, both Breaking Bad and Death of a Salesman encourage audiences to question what they are willing to sacrifice for success and whether the dreams they are chasing are truly their own or merely illusions imposed by society.
